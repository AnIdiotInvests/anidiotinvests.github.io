I am 27, and it took me from 18 to 24 to even remotely consider saving for retirement. Let alone for it to be worthy of my time and effort to plan it out. The narrative in my head went like “Why would I not just use the money now rather than when I am ancient?”

I believe I am not alone on this, but I know now I should have started as soon as possible. The moment I realized retirement doesn't have to be when you're too old to do anything fun, I became obsessed. However, this is dependent on whether or not you start soon enough and contribute enough in the right places (something I am still heavily working on). Not only that, but with enough time horizon it does not have to destroy your lifestyle at the moment.

After pondering for a while, I came to three pillar questions which are the base of my plans. These questions not only keep me motivated while people are trying to convince me it’s useless and uncool to save for retirement, but they also answer why the effort will be worth it to me in the end.

## 1. Why do I Want to Retire?

This is the most overlooked and underrated question to ask yourself when thinking about retirement. Yet this is probably the most important, and is fundamentally the primary factor that will dictate success or failure over the long run.

Is it because you are worried about being physically unable to work and fearful of being a burden later on? Or would you rather pursue what interests you in retirement?

If you are simply saving for retirement just because someone told you to, or out of fear, I would say you should reconsider your reasoning. This is not so that you can justify saving as unjustified, it’s actually the exact opposite. You need a real, tangible and steadfast reason to force yourself to stash away a large portion of your income and just pretend like it does not exist for the majority or at least a large portion of your life.

It is simply human nature to change course when things become difficult or boring in the long-term. Before you get into any number crunching or saving strategies, and especially investing and risk management. For the love of God, have a sound reason to not take the money out of your 401k and/or ROTH to buy your dream house and/or car before you are ready to afford it.

## 2. What do I Plan to be Doing When I Retire?

Now that you actually have a reason to retire and actually look forward to it, this is where you plan out how much you really need based on what you are planning on doing in retirement. Not only using a calculator and telling yourself that you can live on 20 grand a year 30 years from now. Put some time and effort into thinking about what gets your blood pumping! Is it travel? Golf? An assorted collection of Legos?

This type of planning might seem irrelevant or impossible at first, but it truly has to matter to you in the end or you will get burnt out, or even worse not being able to continue doing it after enough time passes and have nothing to fall back on.

Then ask yourself, after paying my bills can I still afford to do these things INDEFINITELY without any lame strings attached? Traveling to every golf course around the world may be expensive, but it's not impossible. However, could you do it for 20 years? Doubtful at best. You need more than just one thing, and you need it to be within a realistic budget. 

## 3. When do I Want to Retire?

Every good thing comes at a cost, even the richest of the rich know this. Being overwhelmingly wealthy can be extremely lonely and cause everything to feel pointless. It's like becoming overpowered in a video game, if you don’t have a goal or mission what was the point of gaining that power? Insane amounts of money will not solve your problems, it is how you use the money you have that has the potential to remove major obstacles stopping you from doing what you want with YOUR life.

Don’t look at saving for retirement like losing a chunk of income, look at it like leveling up and gaining power levels as you go. 

Lastly, make the date you plan to quit working an acceptable timeframe to you and your current and future income. If after running the numbers your goals do not seem possible, then find a way to generate some income to supplement your planned lifestyle.

## How I Plan to Retire

There are not many options here in my opinion sadly, unless you make a ridiculous amount of money. Either save a ton of money or invest some and hope it works out. I know that sounds terrifying, but that’s just the way it is now, and the sooner you start and the more you can give up the more likely success becomes.

The general consensus is that if you do not make enough to simply save for retirement it’s almost impossible without investing in the right places and ways. This is where a lot of misconceptions will start to creep up on people. Like investing in extremely risky assets to make a lot more money short-term than a more logical long-term investing strategy. Don’t fall for the traps, do your due diligence and find what works for you.

In the image above, you can see I plan to start my hypothetical retirement in the passive income 'Start Phase'. I hope to be generating enouph income on my own that I would not have to work if I did not desire to. I plan to continue working during this time to supplement my 'End Phase' further though. In this situation since I am not completly dependent on my job for lifestyle stability, I can switch between income sources freely. In other words, working for fun.

Obviously this will not work for some as the accumulation phase would take so long that the start phase would overlap the end phase. The point is, however, that I have a plan, and I plan to execute it without mercy. If you take nothing else from this article, just know that having a solid plan then adapting to it without breaking the fundamental reason behind it is the most important step.
