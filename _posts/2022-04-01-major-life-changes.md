I am feeling burnt out with my job lately.

Not having a job is a terrifying thought though, the idea of losing my primary stream of income and having to find another.

When anyone brings up the topic of burn out, the most common response is to take a vacation and tough it out, or that your work ethic isn't good enough.

In reality, I like working and putting my best foot forward. I have put in my fair share of 10 hour plus days, and I enjoy giving it my all. The problem is I don't want to put in that level of effort where I currently reside anymore.

This might seem a bit odd for some, and I don't believe everyone should feel the way I do about work either. It is simply the way my brain is wired.

I have been watching some [Gary Vee](https://www.youtube.com/c/garyvee) on YouTube.

Maybe you have heard of him. I can tell he is either very smart or completely insane, or both.

If you have not heard of him, I strongly encourage checking him out. Not because I believe he is some guru, but I enjoy his points about pursuing happiness over immense wealth in all of his videos. I don’t believe the perfect job exists, and I know for a fact there is no such thing as easy money, but there are options.

His advice for people in their twenties is to take more risks while you can and figure out who you are because you only get to do that a few times in your life.

Especially when you have enough time to fix your mistakes if you fail.

Deep down I don't want to take risks. I don't believe in the 'you only live once’ (YOLO) philosophy. Even though I have joked about it several times.

You can make life changes without reverting to knee-jerk reactions to emotional situations. The catch is you need to be in a stable situation first, and that can be insanely hard to do.

I want to go buy a house, a new car, a better computer, have subscriptions to every streaming service, go on ten vacations, and eat out at Fish City Grill every day. Although I might be able to  pay for such a lifestyle for now, in the long run I can't truly afford to.

I don't drop cash on useless stuff unless I have to. I want to keep things humble.

(I can't deny that I do go out and buy a lot of potato chips and craft beer, so I am not perfect. I try to keep this to a minimum though. It's my guilty pleasure.)

Hopefully my future ambition can be matched by enough liquid assets to fuel it. This way I can pursue opportunities while still having a humble and patient mindset. The takeaway is saving enough money that you can afford to make stupid mistakes and I have made a few to say the least.

Having an emergency fund and a frugal-ish lifestyle affords people the ability to take some chances and look for opportunities in new places.

Jumping ship while living paycheck to paycheck would be ludicrous unless absolutely necessary.

In contrast, an abusive situation, disease, or something horrible would justify changing it regardless of money in my opinion!

(Not saying that is easy, it may very well be the most difficult topic in life to change.)

## Getting 'Whys' In Alignment With Reality

I am not happy with my current job situation, but I will always be grateful for it. It’s not a bad job by any means. I owe my entire career to this company, and the people who work there are as nice as can be.

I have been working at my job for nearly four years, but I feel like it’s time to move on.

On top of that, I lost my mentor in my last position change. I'm not saying he died or anything like that, we are simply not on the same project anymore. 

I was on a project that I truly enjoyed and learned something new almost every day. But the best part was my mentor was teaching me how to do many of these things with a decade of experience to do them right. I felt as though I had real control over my career and progress because of this.

He was probably the most important mentor I've had in my career so far.

He didn't just teach me how to do the basics of my job, but also a lot about work ethic and work politics. Some of his teachings have helped me not be offended personally when something goes wrong and is simply business-related.

When I was removed from that project my mentor went along with it to a different project. I tried to stay in touch but I suppose I’m socially awkward and he's got a wife and kids, while I'm a cave dweller with a girlfriend.

So not enough in common to stay in touch over I guess. Which is why I want to move on, I’m not learning anything new anymore.

## Final Thoughts

As a parting thought, a good analogy for what's pushing me to feel this way. Maybe even a good analogy for life, in general, would be Minecraft.

Ridiculous, I know.

The longer you play Minecraft the more resources that you collect and the more time you can spend building larger pointless structures and advancing your player.

At least for me, (excluding when I play creative mode with friends) after a few hours I become bored building a giant castle, and I never actually finish the game by killing the [Ender Dragon](https://minecraft.fandom.com/wiki/Ender_Dragon).

Do what's required so you can pursue your dragon, not an empty castle.

Do this with grace. There is a balance to be struck when taking on a boss. You can't fight a damn dragon if you're not at least wearing some diamond armor and have a nice sword.

In the real world, it is the pursuit of happiness, not castles that we should care about.

I hope anyone who reads this and is going through a similar job situation, please realize you are not crazy. You are simply discovering that what you are doing as a career may not be right for you, and it is not personal against your company and colleagues.

I want to go out and learn how to be my own person, and understand myself more. Gain some self-awareness as they say.

(Not sure who ‘they’ is, but I will roll with it.)

If I'm lucky and have some entrepreneurial talent, perhaps I can build something that generates income that I could claim as my own.
