A major internal conflict has dawned on me lately, a bit of an existential crisis really.

I was watching a YouTube video about millennial money. It was CNBC Millennial Money specifically, and they featured [Graham Stephen](https://www.youtube.com/c/GrahamStephan).

It said that he made 6.1 million dollars a year! If you don’t know who Graham is I would highly recommend checking him out on YouTube for his real estate content.

I'm pretty sure that's net income too, so he's obviously grossing much more than that before taxes.

I mean, 6.1 million is an obscene amount of money right? And it suddenly dawned on me that no matter how hard I work or how much time I put into my current job it would be virtually irrelevant in comparison.

> In my current situation, It’s mathematically impossible to make as much money in my life as Graham makes in one year.

Even if I dropped every cent into low-cost index funds and waited until I was 59 ½, it would hardly make a dent.

Then if you throw inflation on top of that, even if I did reach 6.1 million, it would not be 6.1 million in today's money…

## Being Realistic

I realize how ridiculous it is to compare myself to other people’s income, but I am struggling with that idea. I think this feeling, even before watching this video, is why I have started to tilt my investments toward income.

Keep in mind I would never dig into my 401k, Roth IRA, or primary savings to fund any of this.

I have run the numbers, and I am investing the recommended amount, and then some, to retire later on. If you cannot maintain your existing bare minimum lifestyle after investing money into a strategy like this, then look no further.

There is a high probability you will miss out on long-term gain with income investing.

Anyways, I've fallen for the concept of income investing because I feel if there is a way I can generate more cash flow without having to trade my time for it, then that makes more sense to me.

Then I can invest in more long-term wealth by starting businesses or shooting for other jobs once I have a realistic set of income streams without the 60+ hour weeks permanently attached to it.

> I simply want to free up time to pursue other opportunities.

That doesn't take away from the fact that many income investments are extremely volatile and unpredictable. So there are a lot of risks involved.

In the end, I think a well-diversified portfolio for income that shoots for around 5 to 6% yearly yields is not an unreasonable objective. So if I had a reasonable amount of money invested, the returns would be comparable to a side hustle. At least on paper.

## My Hypothetical Portfolio

Attempting to get the best of both worlds for investing such as income and growth usually just results in overall underperformance.

However, in my case, I am not trying to outperform the market and I don’t want growth, I already have cash and my 401k and Roth IRA is optimized to the extent desired. I want more cash flow to help pay bills.

So with this perspective, we can cheat a little bit, and look into different assets which would normally be off-limits for any sane investor with a long time horizon.

For example, I would never buy assets (in bulk) such as bonds, REITs, and options ETFs if I was worried about a 15+ year time horizon.

Keep in mind any funds listed here may be actively managed and what's going on under the hood can have exposure to more risk than they appear on the surface.

## What Are Covered Calls?

As you may have noticed, a portion of this portfolio has ‘Covered Calls’ in the ETF titles. These are options ETFs.

More specifically they are [Buy-Write](https://www.investopedia.com/terms/b/buy-write.asp) (Covered Call) Options ETFs.

Options are a way for people who don’t know what they are doing, and professionals who think they know what they are doing to lose lots of cash.

That said, there is potential to make money using options, it’s just complicated, and not a get-rich-quick scheme like some ‘GURUS’ try to sell courses on.

To keep things simple you can either write an option or buy an option.

Writing an option implies you are selling the contract to someone else, collecting a premium then hoping the outcome is in your favor.

Where buying the options is the exact opposite, you buy the contract from someone, ie, give them the premium and hope it tilts in your favor and make some profit in the process.

Options give you the ability to either sell or buy an agreement which gives the purchaser of the option the right to sell or buy the asset defined in the agreement at a predefined price.

In my case, I am referring to ETFs when performing the options trading to passively collect premiums for me. Options can and do become much more complicated, but that's outside the scope of this post.

### Should I Do This Myself?

I have never written or bought options on my own because I'm an idiot.

And an idiot and his money part ways quickly, let me tell you.

Leaving it to a fund manager is probably not much, if at all better. At least if the fund has some historical evidence of success that I most definitely do not, at least I can run with that.

## Final Thoughts

My goal is not to live off of income investments as soon as possible indefinitely. Instead, the goal is to increase my current cashflow beyond the limitations of trading my time for it.

The past few years have revealed how important redundant income would be in the event of job loss or when additional income is needed.

I can live off my long-term investments in retirement when I need to later on. Which is what most people want to do anyway.
