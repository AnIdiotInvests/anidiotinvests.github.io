## Performance Breakdown

On a slightly more serious note, if you are not familiar with growth ETFs here is a simple explanation. Growth ETFs are funds that contain stocks that are expected to grow faster due to rapid company expansion and earnings. Emphasis on expected.

Please be aware that while all of these ETFs are centered around growth, many of them follow different underlying indexes which represent different fund goals. I am simply looking at raw performance and volatility.

People seem to think that just because it has ‘growth’ in the name that the fund is somehow better overall. Instead, please do your due diligence on what is happening under the hood before you add anything to your portfolio.

At first, I was only going to cover my top three, but that's not fair to anyone. There are other juicy growth options to look at other than SCHG and VUG, but I did make a list based on AUM and set the cut-off at about 3 Billion-ish to keep it relevant. Also, don’t worry, I will use your precious QQQ, which follows the NASDAQ 100, as a benchmark for some of my comparisons.

Let's weed through the underperformers with simple vibe checks. EFG lost badly to S&P 500, Russell 2000, and NASDAQ 100. So I killed him right off the bat. (I realize that is not the purpose of that fund, we can come back to it another day if you want).

Everything else in this list beats the Russell 2000 in the five-year and one-year graphs or simply had a negligible difference. As I progress, I will show the losers first, otherwise, there is so much going on in the graph you can’t see anything.

{% include image-caption.html image="sp-500-comp-5-year-after.png" caption="S&P 500 comparison 5y graph" %}

In this graph, the underperformance comes from IJT, IJK, IWO, and VBK. There are periods of great outperformance over the S&P 500, and they are not terrible funds. Although I do not believe this kind of performance is worth it when comparing it directly to other options in the same category.

If you wanted S&P 500 level performance, and your underlying strategy is based on something else other than explicitly making better returns, then why use growth funds? Simply go with an S&P 500 index fund with more data to go on. Even if they do have periods of better performance, currently or in the past, why take on the extra risk? Just use something else.

From this point on, everybody beats the S&P 500 in the five-year graph. But what about the one-year graph? I think going into the six-month graph will defeat the purpose of these funds, as the expected volatility and potential downside are likely to be worse in short-term spurts.

{% include image-caption.html image="sp-500-comp-1-year-after.png" caption="S&P 500 comparison 1y graph" %}

I'm not removing every loser on this graph under the S&P 500 because most have a smaller difference in only one year. My only concerns are with VOT and IWP which are both under the goose egg return for the one-year graph. Scary to think you could be sitting well above 10% with something else in this category. It could be a red flag for severe drawdowns in my opinion.

Now on to the Nasdaq 100 comparisons, which turned out exactly like I would have expected, and the QQQ fans can cheer along with me. 

However, I want to bring up a major misconception floating around QQQ. It states that QQQ is strictly a growth fund. This is not true, QQQ is a NASDAQ 100 index fund that happens to overlap many of the popular growth funds and has performed well because of this.

Keep in mind, there is no guarantee this will continue to be the case. Luckily it makes a fantastic benchmark because it has done so freaking well. 

{% include image-caption.html image="first-nas-100-comp-rpg-iusg.png" caption="Nasdaq 100 5y comparison graph" %}

I know what you are thinking, raw performance is not the only factor to consider, but RPG gets RPG’d in the face in comparison to its brethren. Then after removing RPG, you will find IUSG at the bottom in green with a bit less return, which is hard to overlook. 

On this next graph, I want you to look at how tightly correlated these options are. You almost can’t see anything to do with the overlap and I had to hover over the graph to bring up the exact value. This is why people say not to buy the same types of funds.

You do not accomplish anything if all your funds have the same junk in them. It actually can hurt your portfolio by over concentrating your investment into a small selection of stocks in only a few volatile sectors.

## Big Brain Volatility Comparison Time

In case you are wondering, yes, I’m an idiot on the internet using free tools and screenshots of those same tools to try and make a case. However, I do see a point to be made here. That is, looking into the past can make you feel like you made huge mistakes, but in reality, if you're not losing the money you're doing better than most (unlike me). Especially if you are in the 100%+ gain territory for the past 2 years (also unlike me).

It may be a good idea to add growth ETFs to your portfolio if you feel like you know what you are doing (again, also unlike me), but be cautious. The occasionally higher volatility and risk could hurt you a bit down the road if you are not properly diversified. Here are some more astrology charts to clarify.

{% include image-caption.html image="10-day-rolling-close-to-close-volatility-growth.png" caption="Close to close volatility graph" %}

{% include image-caption.html image="current-close-to-close-volatitlity-growth.png" caption="Close to close volatility graph" %}

Hopefully, you see the volatility is comparable to other popular index funds like VTI, VOO, and QQQ. With occasionally 9-ish% (give or take) higher volatility, which means potentially scarier drawdown lows in comparison. This is a good reason not to go all-in if you are risk-averse. Not to mention many of these ETFs are heavily concentrated in the technology sector. Even though there are clearly far riskier things with far worse drawdowns you can invest your money in, I will stay clear of over concentration.

For my main picks, I am a fan of SCHG and VUG, but my god, MGK and IWF have some serious upside on the graphs. I don’t currently own them because they overlap my current portfolio, but from the perspective of raw performance then that’s what I would and may in the future go with. However, if current trends continue QQQ has the most impressive bull run of anything I have ever seen beyond leveraged funds, just an absolutely dumb 700%+ level gain for a time.
