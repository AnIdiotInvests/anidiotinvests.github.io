The Fed Rate Basics

Date: 19 June 2025 | Author: Josh The Destroyer

Jerome H. Powell

Overview

  • What usually happens when the federal funds rate goes up or down historically and why you should care.
  • Why would the Federal Reserve "The Fed" want to do this anyway?
  • Did Jerome Powell or the federal funds rate hurt you? Same here. But guess what, no one cares, the rate will go into effect when the Fed feels like it and the market will react accordingly.

What is the "Fed Rate"

The Federal Funds rate is the interest rate set by the Federal Reserve, aka "The Fed". This "rate" serves as the benchmark "interest rate" for the U.S. economy and typically dictates how virtually all banks, businesses, and even the government issue loans in accordance with this rate.

Examples For Context:

  • Mortgages
  • Credit cards
  • Car loans
  • Treasury yields (one way the government gives loans)
  • The list goes on and on but I am too lazy to put that here.

You can probably infer, changing this rate gives "The Fed" lots of control over the flow of money in the overall economy.

For example, if our boy Jerome Powell snorted a line and raised the fed rate to say 15% getting a loan on a house would also most likely be around 15% or higher from that point on until it changes. And there is basically nothing you could do about it unless you already locked in a 2.8% interest rate on your mortgage before the hike you lucky SOB...

To simplify it further, think of the rate changes like a gas pedal and the economy is a car:

  • Raising rates --> Makes borrowing more expensive which will slow down certain aspects of the economy. (Lifting off the pedal, less fuel)
  • Lowering Rates --> Makes borrowing cheaper, which will speed up the economy and promote more spending. (Pushing down the pedal, more fuel)

Inflation

One of the main reasons the Fed will change rates is to get the inflation rate to what they consider normal, roughly 2-ish%. When the rate of inflation goes above 2-3-ish% then you can bet the Fed will at least consider raising the rate in accordance. Then, in the opposite direction, if the economy slows down too much, and there are fears of a recession or we go into a recession, then the rate may be lowered.

Again to simplify, think gas pedal in response to inflation:

  • Faster Economy/Higher Inflation --> May Raise Rates In Response (Foot off the gas bro)
  • Slower Economy/Lower Inflation --> May Lower Rates In Response (Pedal to the metal)
